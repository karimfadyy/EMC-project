A Timeline of Discovery

The Egyptian Museum in Cairo stands as a testament to Egypt's rich and enduring history. From its inception in the 19th century to its current status as a world-renowned institution, the museum has played a pivotal role in preserving and showcasing the treasures of ancient Egypt. Below is a detailed timeline highlighting the key milestones in the museum's storied past.

1835

In 1835, the Egyptian government took a significant step towards preserving its ancient heritage by establishing the first service dedicated to protecting antiquities. This initiative marked the beginning of organized efforts to safeguard Egypt's rich archaeological treasures, which had been subject to looting and unauthorized exports. The move demonstrated a growing awareness of the importance of cultural preservation and laid the groundwork for future institutions dedicated to Egyptology.

1858

In 1858, French archaeologist Auguste Mariette was appointed as the first officer to oversee Egypt's antiquities, marking the establishment of the Antiquities Department. Recognizing the need for a dedicated space to house Egypt's vast array of ancient artifacts, Mariette founded the first museum in Bulaq, situated in a converted warehouse along the Nile. This museum served as the initial repository for many significant discoveries, reflecting Mariette's dedication to collecting and preserving Egypt's antiquities within the country.

1878

In 1878, a devastating flood of the Nile River severely damaged the Bulaq Museum, highlighting the vulnerability of the collection's location. This disaster underscored the necessity for a more secure and suitable facility to house Egypt's invaluable artifacts. The event catalyzed discussions and plans for relocating the museum to a safer environment, ensuring better protection for the nation's heritage.

1891

Following the flood, the museum's collections were moved in 1891 to a former royal palace in the Giza district of Cairo. This relocation provided a more stable environment for the artifacts and allowed for better organization and display. The Giza location served as an interim solution while plans for a permanent museum building were developed, reflecting the ongoing commitment to preserving Egypt's antiquities.

1902

The current Egyptian Museum building in Tahrir Square was officially opened in 1902. Designed by French architect Marcel Dourgnon, the neoclassical structure was constructed by the Italian company of Giuseppe Garozzo and Francesco Zaffrani. This purpose-built museum was the first of its kind in the Middle East and North Africa, symbolizing a significant advancement in the preservation and exhibition of ancient artifacts.

1922

The discovery of Tutankhamun's tomb in 1922 by Howard Carter brought unprecedented attention to the Egyptian Museum. The tomb's treasures, including the iconic golden mask, were transferred to the museum, significantly enhancing its collection. This event captivated the world and solidified the museum's status as a premier institution for Egyptology, attracting scholars and tourists alike.

1954

In 1954, a portion of the museum's garden, which extended to the Nile, was repurposed for the construction of the Cairo Municipality Building. This development reduced the museum's outdoor space but also reflected the evolving urban landscape of Cairo. Despite these changes, the museum continued to adapt and maintain its role as a guardian of Egypt's ancient heritage.

2004

Wafaa El Saddik was appointed as the first female director general of the Egyptian Museum in 2004. Her leadership marked a progressive step in the institution's history, emphasizing inclusivity and modernization. Under her guidance, the museum undertook various initiatives to enhance its exhibitions and educational programs, aiming to engage a broader audience and promote cultural understanding.

2011

During the Egyptian Revolution of 2011, the museum faced significant challenges, including break-ins and the destruction of two mummies. Several artifacts were damaged, and around 50 objects were stolen. The incident highlighted the vulnerabilities of cultural institutions during times of political unrest. Restoration efforts were promptly initiated, and by 2013, many of the damaged items were recovered and displayed in a special exhibition titled "Damaged and Restored."

2021

In April 2021, the museum played a central role in the "Pharaohs' Golden Parade," a grand event that transported 22 royal mummies from the Egyptian Museum to the National Museum of Egyptian Civilization. This historic procession showcased Egypt's commitment to honoring its ancient rulers and preserving their legacies. The event garnered international attention and highlighted the museum's ongoing relevance in the cultural landscape.

Present

Today, the Egyptian Museum houses over 120,000 items, making it one of the most comprehensive collections of ancient Egyptian artifacts in the world. The museum continues to serve as a vital center for research, education, and cultural preservation. As Egypt prepares for the opening of the Grand Egyptian Museum, the Egyptian Museum in Tahrir Square remains a symbol of the nation's enduring connection to its ancient past.
